The Oesper Collections in the History of Chemistry, University of Cincinnati
Notes from the Oesper Collections

These occasional notes will highlight items and topics of interest in the Oesper Collections in the History of Chemistry.

No. 11: The Curious Case of Lloyd’s Artificial Man, November ⁄ December 2011.
This issue, based on the Oesper collections' archival records, describes of the interactions during the 1920s between a well-known Cincinnati pharmacist and the 5th head of the UC Chemistry Department.
No. 10: The Chemical Uses of Clay Tobacco Pipes, September ⁄ October 2011.
Many common everyday objects have been put to use in the chemical laboratory, but few would guess that clay tobacco pipes would be among them.
No. 9: The Bunge Short-Beam Balance, July ⁄ August 2011.
How the German engineer, Paul Bunge (1839-1888) revolutionized balance design through his introduction in 1866 of the first "short-beam" analytical balance. Illustrations and descriptions in this issue highlight a few of the more than 50 chemical balances found in the Oesper Apparatus Collections.
No. 8: Plagiarizing the Professor, May ⁄ June 2011.
A tale of the two editions owned by the Oesper Collections of the classic early 18th-century chemistry textbook by the famous Dutch chemist, Herman Boerhaave, involving student plagiarism, law suits, and the famous British lexicographer Samuel Johnson.
No. 7: A Rookwood History of Chemistry, April 2011.
A description with illustrations of the unique ceramic drinking fountain, made of one-of-a-kind faience tiles manufactured by the famous Rookwood Pottery Company, located inside the main entrance to the building now known as "Old Chemistry."  The fountain was a gift to the University from the Senior Engineering Class of 1921 and is designed to symbolically embody, when read from top downwards, the evolution of modern chemistry.
No. 6: The Single-Lens Microscope, March 2011.
A description of some of the examples of 19th and early 20th century single-lens microscopes in the Oesper Collections, and the important role played by the single-lens or simple microscope in scientific discovery.
No. 5: Herbert Hoover and the History of Chemistry, February 2011.
The history of the events surrounding the 1929 acquisition of a first edition of the only English-language version of an important 16th-century Latin scientific work translated by an American mining engineer and future President of the United States.
No. 4: Elijah Slack, January 2011.
The life and scientific career of the first Professor of Chemistry in Cincinnati, Elijah Slack, who was Princeton's first Vice-President before coming to Cincinnati and holding positions at Cincinnati College and the Medical College of Ohio.
No. 3: The Craig Countercurrent Distribution Train, December 2010.
The Oesper Apparatus Collection has recently acquired a rare, surviving example of a 30-cell Craig Countercurrent Distribution Train. This apparatus, described in 1949 by Dr. Lyman C. Craig of the Rockefeller Institute for Medical Research, formed a key advance in liquid-liquid extraction techniques. Dr. Robley Williams, a former student of Craig, drove the unit across country from his home in Colorado to donate it to the Oesper Collections.
No. 2: Oesper's Salt, November 2010.
This issue of Notes from the Oesper Collections describes the introduction in 1947 by Ralph Edward Oesper of a new oxidimetric standard now known as "Oesper's Salt."
No. 1: Found Tucked in a Book, October 2010.
The first issue of Notes from the Oesper Collections describes the discovery, tucked into the pages of rare books, of previously unknown photographs of notable American chemists.
